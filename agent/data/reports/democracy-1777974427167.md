## Report: The Erosion of Democratic Participation and the Normalization of Authoritarian Governance

### Executive Summary

This report examines five interconnected dimensions of democratic erosion in the United States as of May 2026, drawing on legislative analysis, fact-checking, investigative reporting, campaign finance data, and government communications. The most significant current expression of voter suppression is the SAVE America Act (S. 1383 / H.R. 7296), a Republican-backed bill requiring documentary proof of citizenship to register and photo identification to vote. Research shows the bill could block more than 21 million eligible American citizens from voting, with disproportionate impacts on younger voters, voters of color, and married women. In the realm of dark money, 15 years after the *Citizens United* decision, outside spending reached approximately $4.5 billion in the 2024 election cycle, with dark money contributions hitting $1.4 billion. On norm erosion, the Trump administration has launched an unprecedented campaign to undermine election integrity — weaponizing the Department of Justice, pardoning January 6 insurrectionists, targeting law firms and election officials for retaliation, dismantling election security infrastructure, and shifting the DOJ Civil Rights Division away from protecting voting rights toward pursuing voter roll purges. On authoritarian admiration, while direct scraped content was limited, the broader pattern of governance described in the available sources reflects a clear embrace of executive power unconstrained by democratic checks and balances. On judicial activism, evidence from the reports documents how conservative-dominated courts and DOJ actions are bypassing legislative processes to enforce conservative policy outcomes.

### Findings

#### 1. VOTER SUPPRESSION: The SAVE America Act and "Election Integrity" as a Pretext for Disenfranchisement

**The SAVE America Act would require documentary proof of citizenship to register and a narrow list of acceptable photo IDs to vote.** The bill (H.R. 7296, introduced in the House on January 30, 2026, and S. 1383 in the Senate) passed the House in February 2026 after weeks of debate on the Senate floor in March and April. As of late April 2026, the bill has stalled in the Senate, blocked by the filibuster, and an attempt to attach it to a budget reconciliation bill also failed. [1]

**More than 21 million American citizens lack ready access to the required documents.** A 2023 survey by the Brennan Center for Justice found that more than 9% of voting-age Americans (21.3 million people) would not be able to "quickly find" documents such as a passport, birth certificate, or naturalization papers. More than 3.8 million people do not have those documents at all. Roughly half of Americans do not have a passport. Millions lack access to a paper copy of their birth certificate. [1][2]

**The Bipartisan Policy Center found even higher estimates of potential disenfranchisement.** Analyzing the 2024 Survey on the Performance of American Elections, the Bipartisan Policy Center found that 12% of registered voters (the equivalent of 28.4 million citizens of voting age) lacked either a valid passport or a birth certificate they could easily find along with a valid government-issued photo ID. [2]

**The bill's photo ID requirement is more restrictive than the laws of 49 states.** The SAVE America Act provides a narrow list of acceptable IDs — state-issued driver's license or ID, U.S. passport, military ID, or tribal ID with an expiration date. It prohibits the use of student IDs (even those issued by state universities) and accepts tribal IDs only with an expiration date, even though many tribal IDs do not contain expiration dates. The National Conference of State Legislatures confirmed that the bill is "stricter" than most state voter ID laws; currently 36 states have some form of voter ID law, but 13 states accept non-photo identification such as a bank statement, and states like Georgia accept a broader range of documents including student IDs from public colleges, expired state driver's licenses, and free voter ID cards. [1][2]

**The bill would place an unfunded burden on election officials and expose them to legal risk.** Local officials would be left to decide whether a voter lacking specified documents has done enough to prove citizenship. Officials who make an honest mistake could face civil and criminal penalties. The bill also imposes criminal penalties on election officials who register someone "who fails to present documentary proof of United States citizenship." [1]

**The in-person registration requirement creates additional barriers.** The bill requires that documentary proof of citizenship be presented "in person" to an election official when registering or re-registering (such as after moving or changing one's name). Eliza Sweren-Becker of the Brennan Center stated that this in-person requirement would be "especially hard" for "working parents, people with disabilities, elderly voters, voters who live in rural areas." [2]

**The affidavit alternative is uncertain and potentially unreliable.** The bill provides that an applicant who cannot provide required documents may sign an attestation under penalty of perjury, but the election official must also sign an affidavit "swearing or affirming the applicant sufficiently established United States citizenship." Walter Olson of the Cato Institute noted that the process hinges on guidance from the Election Assistance Commission, saying: "One of the reasons I am critical of the bill is that I don't believe we should be asked to take it on faith that the EAC will issue practical guidance in good faith." Sweren-Becker noted that the discretion left to local officials, combined with the threat of personal criminal liability, will pressure them to construe the affidavit method "very, very, very narrowly." [2]

**Voters could be purged from rolls without adequate notice.** One provision states that a state "shall remove an individual who is not a citizen of the United States from the official list of eligible voters... at any time upon receipt of documentation or verified information." This provision does not specify that notice must be given before removal. Walter Olson noted that this means "anyone, including the federal government or some private person or group, can send 'documentation or verified information' to a state that a certain person... on its voter rolls are not U.S. citizens. The state then 'shall' remove them." Justin Levitt, a constitutional law professor, said the bill "seems to contemplate at least some people being kicked off the rolls without being told." [2]

**The bill would use a flawed DHS database to identify supposed noncitizens.** The SAVE America Act directs states to submit voter rolls to DHS's Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements (SAVE) program, which has been shown to have flaws and has wrongly identified citizens as noncitizens. [1][2]

**The White House defends the bill as broadly popular and necessary for election security.** In a February 10, 2026 press release, the White House cited Pew Research Center polling showing 83% of Americans favor requiring photo ID to vote (including 95% of Republicans and 71% of Democrats), and Gallup polling showing 84% support for photo ID and 83% support for requiring proof of citizenship when registering. The release characterized opponents as "Radical Left Democrat politicians" and cited CNN's Harry Enten saying "Voter ID is NOT controversial in this country." [5]

**All available evidence shows noncitizen voting is vanishingly rare.** The Brennan Center notes that evidence from the Trump administration itself indicates only American citizens vote and exceptions are vanishingly rare. States that have combed through voter rolls — like Louisiana and Utah — have repeatedly confirmed this fact. The Center for Election Innovation & Research found that allegations of noncitizen registration or voting stem from mischaracterizations of voter data, and that existing election safeguards are effective. [1]

**The Make Elections Great Again Act, a companion bill, includes even more restrictive provisions.** These include: requiring proof of residence as well as citizenship (disproportionately affecting the 9% of the population who move within a state each year); mandated voter roll purges every 30 days (eliminating the 90-day quiet period protecting voters from mistaken removal before Election Day); prohibition of universal mail voting (ending the principal method of voting in eight states and Washington, DC); and the same restrictive photo ID requirement. [1]

#### 2. DARK MONEY: Defending Unlimited, Anonymous Spending as "Free Speech"

**The *Citizens United* decision (2010) unleashed unlimited election spending by corporations, unions, nonprofits, and individuals.** The ruling overturned the prohibition on independent political expenditures by corporations and unions in the 2002 Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act. *SpeechNow.org v. FEC* (2010) allowed unlimited contributions to independent expenditure committees (super PACs), which became the primary vehicle for outside spending. [4]

**Outside spending grew from $574 million in 2008 to approximately $4.5 billion in 2024.** In the 2008 election cycle (the last before *Citizens United*), outside spending totaled $574 million. By 2012 it more than doubled to nearly $1.3 billion. By 2020 it reached $3.3 billion, and by 2024 it approached $4.5 billion. Most of this money was spent by super PACs. Super PAC spending itself grew from $62.6 million in 2010 to $622.7 million in 2012 (the first presidential cycle after *Citizens United*) to over $4.1 billion in 2024. [4]

**The top 100 individual donors now account for 14–16% of all federal election spending.** In 2008, the top 100 individual donors contributed $80.9 million, or 1.5% of the $5.3 billion spent. Their share climbed dramatically through 2016 and has since settled in the 14–16% range. The biggest donors in 2010 gave $7.6 million; by 2024, everyone in the top 10 gave at least $42.3 million, with Elon Musk leading at $280 million. Michael Bloomberg was the only top-10 donor from 2010 who remained there in 2024. [4]

**Dark money spending reached $1.4 billion in 2024.** Politically active nonprofit organizations (501(c)(4) groups) are not required to disclose their donors but can give unlimited sums to super PACs. Dark money contributions and direct spending peaked at $359 million in 2012, then jumped to $734 million in 2020 and $1.4 billion in 2024 (pending year-end reports). As OpenSecrets notes, "barring the unlikely enactment of new laws governing limits on campaign donations and disclosure of those contributions, elections are only going to become more expensive and less transparent." [4]

**63% of Americans disagree with the *Citizens United* decision.** Polling by Issue One found that 63% of Americans — including 73% of Democrats, 53% of Republicans, and 61% of independents — disagreed with the Supreme Court's decision that corporations and labor unions have a First Amendment right to spend unlimited money to influence elections. **The Arizona Supreme Court is weighing a challenge to the Voters Right to Know Act,** a measure that won 70% of the vote in 2022 requiring disclosure of most anonymous campaign spending. The challenge was filed by the Goldwater Institute, a libertarian legal firm, arguing the law violates the Arizona Constitution by eliminating free speech rights to donate anonymously. [This information comes from search results, not directly scraped pages — noted as contextual.]

**A Montana ballot initiative aimed at undoing *Citizens United* was blocked by the state Attorney General.** The Montana Attorney General ruled the anti-dark money ballot initiative had too many parts, and noted the amendment could violate federal legal precedent providing corporations with First Amendment protections including the right to free speech through political donations. [2]

#### 3. AUTHORITARIAN ADMIRATION and STRONGMAN GOVERNANCE

**Direct scraped content on praise for foreign autocrats was limited** — the Foreign Policy article "The Trump-Orban Strongman Era Has Peaked" and the NYT article on Trump and MAGA embracing Orban failed to load during scraping. However, the Brennan Center report documents patterns of governance consistent with authoritarian admiration and strongman-style rule.

**The Trump administration has embraced leaders who subvert democratic institutions.** The Brennan Center notes that "many of this administration's political appointees have continuously challenged the results of the 2020 election or otherwise undermined elections." At least four members of the president's cabinet are election deniers. [3]

**The administration has taken actions that mirror authoritarian playbooks.** These include: pardoning approximately 1,500 January 6 rioters who violently attacked the Capitol to keep the president in power after he lost the 2020 election; directing the DOJ to support a convicted election official (Tina Peters) who gave unauthorized access to voting equipment; using executive orders to target law firms for representing political adversaries; threatening to prosecute election officials who administered the 2020 election; and creating federal task forces premised on the false notion that U.S. elections are rigged. [3]

**The president has described January 6 defendants as "warriors" and "political prisoners."** He has cast the rioters as victims rather than criminals, and Republican Party events around the country have honored them. Some insurrectionists have discussed regrouping following the pardons, and some have attempted or are considering runs for public office, including seats in Congress. [3]

**The administration has signaled that it will support officials who engage in election subversion.** The DOJ filed a statement of interest supporting Tina Peters' habeas corpus petition, evaluating whether her state prosecution was politically motivated. The president posted on social media describing Peters as an "innocent Political Prisoner" and directed the DOJ to "take all necessary action to help secure" her release. The Brennan Center notes: "These actions could embolden rogue election officials to threaten elections from within. The president and the DOJ are sending a clear message that they will support such officials when they engage in subversive behavior." [3]

#### 4. JUDICIAL ACTIVISM: Bypassing Legislative and Democratic Processes

**The DOJ under the Trump administration has shifted from protecting voting rights to pursuing voter roll purges.** The Civil Rights Division's voting section dwindled from approximately 30 lawyers down to about 6, with an estimated 250 attorneys (approximately 70% of the division's lawyers) leaving the department by the end of May 2025. The division is now led by Maureen Riordan, a former lawyer for the Public Interest Legal Foundation (PILF), a conservative organization that has for years sued election officials to force aggressive voter roll purges. [3]

**The DOJ has sued election administrators to force voter purges.** Under the new leadership, the DOJ sued election administrators in North Carolina in an apparent attempt to purge thousands of eligible voters from the rolls. As many as 200,000 North Carolina voter registration records are missing a driver's license number or the last four digits of a Social Security number — errors by the state, not voters — but the DOJ asked for relief that "would result in the removal of many of these voters from the rolls." The DOJ has also sued an Orange County, California election official for failing to provide unredacted private information about voters he had removed from the rolls. [3]

**The DOJ has dropped every voting rights case it inherited and withdrawn from voting and redistricting suits.** It gutted the civil rights division of career staff, issued new mission statements signaling a move away from protecting marginalized groups' rights and toward rooting out voter fraud (which is "actually extremely rare"). The DOJ also filed statements of interest in Oregon and Illinois supporting conservative activist lawsuits seeking more aggressive voter roll purges. [3]

**The Eighth Circuit ruling has stripped private plaintiffs of the ability to enforce the Voting Rights Act.** The court of appeals for the Seventh Circuit ruled that only the DOJ can bring suits to enforce the Voting Rights Act's central prohibition against discrimination. Since the DOJ has signaled it will not enforce these protections, voters in seven states (Arkansas, Iowa, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, and South Dakota) are "essentially left without the primary protections of the landmark civil rights law." [3]

**Courts have blocked some administration overreach, but the damage persists.** Multiple courts have found that the president's executive orders targeting law firms violate the First Amendment, and two courts preliminarily stopped the president from unlawfully directing an independent agency to add a citizenship document requirement to the federal voter registration form. However, despite the clear illegality of the orders, nine law firms have entered into agreements with the administration, including providing free legal services for causes the president approves of. [3]

**Project 2025, the Heritage Foundation's conservative policy agenda, proposed prosecuting election officials.** The plan proposed charging election officials with criminal conspiracy to injure civil rights for their alleged misapplication of state law to help voters cast ballots during the 2020 election. The Trump administration's actions — including the Weaponization Working Group, the New Jersey Election Integrity Task Force, and the DC Special Unit for Election Accountability — track these proposals. [3]

#### 5. NORM EROSION: Normalizing Refusal to Accept Election Results and Use of State Power Against Opponents

**The Trump administration has launched a "concerted drive to undermine American elections" that is "unprecedented and in some cases illegal."** The Brennan Center's comprehensive expert brief, published August 3, 2025, and updated through February 2026, documents the administration's actions, including: attempting to rewrite election rules to burden voters; targeting or threatening election officials; supporting people who undermine election administration; and retreating from the federal government's role of protecting voters and the election process. [3]

**The president issued a March 2025 executive order attempting to overhaul and exert partial control over election systems.** The order purports to: (1) direct the Election Assistance Commission to mandate that voters show a passport or similar document proving citizenship when registering; (2) force the EAC to rescind all previous certifications of voting equipment and re-certify under amended guidelines; and (3) call on DOGE and DHS to obtain state voter files. A federal court recently stated: "The Constitution vests none of these powers in the President." Two courts have blocked some provisions while litigation proceeds. [3]

**The administration has attempted to interfere with voting equipment.** A DHS official and an individual claiming to work for the administration asked election officials in Colorado for access to their voting equipment. In his first term, the president tried to direct the attorney general, the Department of Defense, and DHS to seize voting machines. [3]

**The administration has weaponized federal agencies against perceived adversaries.** The DOJ's Weaponization Working Group, the New Jersey Election Integrity Task Force, and the DC Special Unit for Election Accountability establish a federal law enforcement approach focused on targeting voters, election officials, perceived political adversaries, and journalists. Political appointees who have spread falsehoods about the 2020 election — Attorney General Pam Bondi, Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon, and FBI Director Kash Patel — have empowered these task forces. Patel has stated: "We're going to come after the people in the media who lied about American citizens, who helped Joe Biden rig presidential elections — we're going to come after you." [3]

**The administration has targeted law firms for representing political adversaries.** The president issued executive orders revoking federal security clearances of six law firms and prohibiting federal contracting with them because they were associated with people who challenged the January 6 attack, attempts to overturn the 2020 election, or election interference investigations. The order targeting Perkins Coie cited the firm's representation of a political opponent and its challenges to restrictive voter ID laws. The order targeting Jenner & Block was based on its employment of Andrew Weissman, who "drew presidential disdain" for participating in the investigation and prosecution of the president's campaign team. Multiple courts found these orders violated the First Amendment and permanently blocked them, but nine law firms entered into agreements with the administration. [3]

**The president pardoned approximately 1,500 January 6 rioters on the first day of his second term.** The Brennan Center notes: "The move green-lights future vigilante efforts to overturn elections, including efforts by the January 6 rioters themselves, by indicating that those who engage in political violence and interfere with the peaceful transfer of power may not only avoid legal consequences but also be treated as heroes." [3]

**The administration has dismantled election security infrastructure.** CISA, the agency responsible for protecting election system cybersecurity, had 17 election security staff put on administrative leave in February 2025. DOGE fired 130 people from CISA (later reinstated but immediately placed on administrative leave). CISA paused all election security activities and cut approximately $10 million in funding to the Center for Internet Security, eliminating the Election Infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Center. The Brennan Center warns: "Scaling back the expertise at CISA and cutting the critical funding it delivers to state and local officials for election security expose U.S. election infrastructure to cyberattacks and physical attacks." [3]

**The administration has threatened to prosecute election officials.** President Trump stated in a March 14, 2025 speech: "What a difference a rigged and crooked election had on our country. And the people who did this to us should go to jail. They should go to jail." The DOJ is reportedly exploring ways to criminally charge election officials for failing to maintain proper cybersecurity — "based not on evidence but on assumptions driven by false claims of rampant voter fraud." DOJ lawyers have reportedly struggled to find any criminal statute to match the administration's demands. [3]

**The threats have already driven election officials from their jobs.** In 2024, 33% of local election officials knew at least one colleague who resigned at least in part due to fear for their safety, of increased threats, or of intimidation. Twenty-one percent stated they were unlikely to continue serving for the 2026 midterms. By 2025, 59% of officials reported fear of political interference in their ability to do their jobs, and 46% were very or somewhat concerned about potential politically motivated investigations. [3]

**The administration has pressured states to turn over voter data.** The DOJ has requested voter lists from at least nine states, and at least two have provided them. The administration conceded in January 2026 that DOGE team members within the Social Security Administration agreed to turn over state voter rolls to an advocacy group seeking to "find evidence of voter fraud and to overturn election results in certain States." Several states that have agreed to turn over data gave the administration "unprecedented latitude to meddle in elections, including the power to tell states to remove specific voters from the rolls and share sensitive, private voter data at will — including with people outside of government." [1][3]

**DOGE has a track record of mishandling sensitive data.** Within minutes after gaining access to the National Labor Relations Board's systems in March 2025, someone with a Russian IP address made several attempts to access the database using one of the department's newly created accounts. The Brennan Center warns that "the fact that foreign adversaries were able to immediately make such attempts indicates that election systems and the data they hold risk being compromised when left in the hands of DOGE." [3]

**The administration has retreated from protecting voting rights.** The DOJ has dropped every voting case in which it had been a plaintiff at the start of the administration and withdrew from several voting and redistricting suits. By the end of May 2025, an estimated 70% of Civil Rights Division lawyers had left the department. Dhillon described the division's new direction as "turning the train around and driving in the opposite direction" from previous Republican administrations' approach. [3]
