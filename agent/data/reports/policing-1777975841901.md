#### 1. BRUTALITY APOLOGIA: The "Split-Second Decision" and "Moment of the Threat" Doctrines

- **Supreme Court Review of the "Moment of the Threat" Doctrine (January 2025)**: The Supreme Court heard oral arguments on January 22, 2025, in a case arising from the 2016 killing of 24-year-old Ashtian Barnes by Harris County Precinct 5 Constable's Office Officer Roberto Felix Jr. during a routine traffic stop in Houston, Texas. Barnes was shot twice in the torso and died at the scene. A grand jury found no probable cause for criminal charges, and the officer was not disciplined. [1]

- **The Narrowed Judicial Lens**: Lower courts had dismissed the family's excessive force lawsuit using the "moment of the threat doctrine," which restricts judicial review to "the precise moment that force was used" rather than the totality of circumstances leading up to it. Judge Patrick Higginbotham of the 5th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals reluctantly authored the opinion for Felix, writing that if he were not bound by the doctrine and could evaluate broader evidence, he "would have concluded that a constitutional violation had taken place." Texas-based District Judge Alfred Bennett similarly criticized the approach for having "effectively stifled a more robust examination of the Fourth Amendment's protections." [1]

- **Officer's Own Account**: In his deposition, Felix said the sequence took "maybe two, three seconds, possibly." He had been a police officer since 2004 and was involved in another fatal shooting in 2007. Felix stated that Barnes' behavior — such as not having his license and rummaging for documents — "kept indicating to me that he was up to something." A gun was later found in the vehicle, but Felix said he did not see signs Barnes was armed at the time of the shooting. [1]

- **The National Divide**: The 12 federal regional appeals courts are split on the doctrine, with four embracing the "moment of the threat" approach and eight rejecting it. A ruling for the Barnes family would void the doctrine nationwide; a ruling for Felix would make it even harder for plaintiffs to bring excessive force claims. [1]

- **Qualified Immunity as an Additional Shield**: Even if the family won on the doctrine, NBC News reporting notes that Felix could still invoke qualified immunity, which courts have "increasingly used to shield police officers." The defense applies when a constitutional violation occurs but the officer was not on notice that the conduct was unlawful. [1]

- **Biden Administration's Position**: The Biden administration filed an amicus brief partly backing the family, with Solicitor General Elizabeth Prelogar arguing that courts "should not categorically disregard prior officer conduct." However, she also stated that because officers must make "split-second judgments in defending themselves and the public from danger," the moment force was used "will generally have primary significance." [1]

- **Contrast with Model Policy**: The Stanford Law School Model Use of Force Policy (February 2025) states that officers "should consider the importance of using their tactical training to maximize their decision time and avoid split-second decision-making," noting that time "allows officers to make more accurate risk assessments." [Search result, cited from law.stanford.edu]

- **Victim's Mother's Testimony**: Janice Hughes, Barnes' mother, who filed the civil rights lawsuit, stated: "I want out of this that my son was a victim. He was never a suspect... I feel like at some point, somebody has to really take this seriously, because it continues to happen and all we get to do is rally, and 'Black Lives Matter,' and nothing changes. Nobody's policing the police." [1]

- **A Cato Institute lawyer** supporting the family called a win for Hughes "of symbolic and material significance." A ruling was expected by the end of June 2025. [1]

#### 2. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY: Codification as a Legislative Priority

- **The Qualified Immunity Act of 2025**: On January 16, 2025, Senator Jim Banks (R-Ind.) introduced the Qualified Immunity Act of 2025. The bill would codify the qualified immunity doctrine into statute, "providing protection for the rights of law enforcement agents to perform their duties in good faith and without fear of extraneous litigation." It also protects governments from liability when their agencies are "found to have acted in good faith." [2]

- **Banks' Framing**: Banks stated: "My legislation will protect those who risk their lives every day to keep our communities safe. Removing qualified immunity would open police departments up to frivolous lawsuits that would effectively bankrupt and defund them. This bill ensures that officers acting in good faith are protected while still holding accountable those who violate clearly established precedent. I will always back the blue!" [2]

- **Co-Sponsors**: The bill's original co-sponsors were Senators Ted Budd (R-NC), Mike Crapo (R-Idaho), Tim Sheehy (R-Mont.), Rick Scott (R-Fla.), Marsha Blackburn (R-Tenn.), Cindy Hyde-Smith (R-Miss.), Markwayne Mullin (R-Okla.), and Jim Risch (R-Idaho). [2]

- **Institutional Endorsements**: The bill was endorsed by the National Fraternal Order of Police, Police Officers' Defense Coalition, National Association of Police Organizations, Indiana State Police Alliance, Indiana Association of Chiefs of Police, National Center for Police Defense, National Association for School Resource Officers, National Troopers Coalition, National Sheriffs' Association, Minneapolis Police and Peace Officers Association, Austin Police Department Association, Wyoming Association of Sheriffs and Chiefs of Police, and the Prince William County Police Association. [2]

- **Police Union Leadership Statements**:
  - **Scott Krueger, Indiana State Police Alliance**: "Our members do not need to be burdened with the fear of frivolous claims as they make difficult split-second decisions while performing their duties." [2]
  - **Chief Kyle Prewitt, Indiana Association of Chiefs of Police**: The act "codifies qualified immunity protections for law enforcement officers, paving the way for them to be proactively engaged with communities and seeking to deter and prevent crime without fear of being exposed to lawsuits for simply doing their jobs." [2]
  - **Patrick Yoes, National President of the Fraternal Order of Police**: "Unless there is existing precedent that squarely governs the facts before the officer, a reasonable officer needs to be afforded a certain degree of discretion to make split-second decisions in situations that could put lives, including their own, at risk. Codifying the qualified immunity doctrine — which has been upheld by the courts for decades — will benefit public safety." [2]
  - **Bill Johnson, Executive Director, National Association of Police Organizations**: "Without it, officers will hesitate or be paralyzed in fear of civil liability for allegedly violating an unknown (and unknowable) civil right." [2]

- **Background Context**: Banks first pushed to codify qualified immunity in 2020 as a House member, "in response to Democrats' crusade to overturn it by including language in their police reform bill aimed at dismantling the doctrine." The bill claims to provide "Accountability Safeguards" that "preserve avenues to hold officers accountable for egregious misconduct." [2]

- **The ABA on Qualified Immunity's True Standard**: The American Bar Association has noted that qualified immunity turns on whether the officer violated "clearly established law" — a standard that "has nothing to do with whether the defendant was actually acting in good faith; all that matters is whether the facts in the case are sufficiently similar to the facts of prior decisions." [Search result]

#### 3. ACCOUNTABILITY OPPOSITION: Police Union Campaigns Against Civilian Oversight

**Hartford, Connecticut (March 2026)**

- **Union Challenge to Civilian Review Board**: The Hartford Police Union, representing officers up to and including captains, filed a labor complaint on January 8, 2026, alleging that the city violated the Municipal Employee Relations Act when it strengthened the Civilian Police Review Board (CPRB) in 2020. The union's Sgt. James Rutkauski stated: "The third-party outside arbitration is illegal." The State Board of Labor Relations was scheduled to hear the case on April 1–2, 2026. [3]

- **Background of the Board**: The CPRB was established in 1992 over police union objections, replacing an earlier Investigative Review Board viewed as "secretive and ineffective." In a 1999 news report, a councilman described the original board as having the power of a "bowl of wet macaroni." [3]

- **2020 Reforms**: After George Floyd's killing, the board was given subpoena power (authorized by new state legislation), a full-time inspector general was created, and the board gained the ability to take misconduct cases to binding arbitration. [3]

- **Union's Position**: Rutkauski argued the city should rewrite the municipal code to restore the CPRB to its "previous role as an advisory board." He claimed the board's investigations are "largely duplicative" of internal affairs and questioned its funding: "There isn't a smoking gun. When was the last time... they dug something up and went, 'Oh my goodness. The Hartford Police Department tried to bury this'? We don't have that type of environment. It doesn't exist." [3]

- **Inspector General's Assessment**: Joseph Lopez, named inspector general in late 2024, stated that the board has "no clear path to enforce subpoenas in the judicial system" and "the sobering truth remains that five years after that ordinance was strengthened, the board has yet to achieve the meaningful impact and genuine accountability that City Council intended." [3]

- **First Arbitration Cases**: In June and August 2025, the board voted to send two cases to arbitration for the first time — one involving an officer's conduct during a domestic disturbance arrest, and another involving three officers' failure to properly investigate an assault. The union's complaint could derail the process. [3]

- **Broader Context**: Police killings in 2026 of two Black men experiencing mental health crises in Hartford had "sparked sustained outcry" from the community. [3]

**Seattle, Washington (March 2026)**

- **Contract Blocks 2017 Reforms**: Seattle's latest contract with the Seattle Police Officers Guild, running through 2027, continues to block accountability measures the City Council approved in 2017. The deal was negotiated by former Mayor Bruce Harrell's administration and approved by the City Council in December 2025 amid a push to hire more officers. [4]

- **Statute of Limitations**: The contract retains a four-year statute of limitations on most misconduct, barring discipline if a complaint isn't made within that window. The 2017 ordinance removed the deadline for dishonesty or excessive force cases, but those changes were never incorporated into the contract. The new agreement lifts the four-year limit only in limited circumstances — criminal allegations or if an officer personally conceals evidence. If someone else hides evidence, discipline can still be barred. [4]

- **Oversight Limits**: The contract caps the number of civilian investigators at the Office of Police Accountability (OPA), bars civilians from serving as sole lead investigators on serious misconduct cases, and prevents OPA from subpoenaing evidence not held by the city (such as private security videos, bank records, and personal text messages). That subpoena authority is already available to other city agencies including the Seattle Ethics and Elections Commission and the Office of Human Rights. [4]

- **Arbitration Instead of Civil Service**: The 2017 law routed disciplinary appeals to a public civil service commission and barred appeals to private arbitrators. However, the current contract "continues to rely heavily on arbitration to resolve disciplinary decisions." This means an officer fired for misconduct can be reinstated after a private arbitration proceeding that can "drag on for years." Jazmyn Clark of the ACLU of Washington said the system sends a message that "discipline is negotiable even for serious violations." [4]

- **Elevated Standard of Review**: The contract retains an "elevated standard of review" for cases that could end in firing, making serious misconduct harder to prove and discipline easier to overturn on appeal. Anne Levinson, the former judge who co-wrote the 2017 law, called this "the largest barrier to accountability." [4]

- **Council Opposition**: Councilmember Rob Saka called OPA's limited subpoena power the contract's "most glaring failure": "Without this critical tool, misconduct investigations can't be completed, trust erodes, and institutions are shielded instead of people." [4]

- **City Journal / Manhattan Institute Defense of Union Protections (2023)**: Writing for the Manhattan Institute's City Journal, Daniel DiSalvo and Michael Oved argued that research on police unions is "mixed" and "caution is in order." They noted that a "central claim" of critics is that unions "protect bad officers and keep them on the beat" through contract provisions that require procedural steps before personnel action, dictate how and when officers can be interviewed, require destruction of disciplinary records, and provide extensive appeal rights. Few records survive of the committee's deliberations during drafting, and the debate in Congress and state legislatures "featured almost no discussion of this provision" — it was "apparently considered noncontroversial at the time."
