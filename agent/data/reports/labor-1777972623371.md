## The Erosion of Labor Protections and Normalization of Worker Exploitation

### Executive Summary
Research into current labor trends (as of May 2026) reveals a systemic shift toward deregulation and the rollback of established worker protections across multiple sectors. Key findings include:
- **Gig Economy & Benefits**: A push for "portable benefits" is being framed as a modernization of the workforce, but critics argue it serves as a mechanism to exclude gig workers from traditional employment rights and stability [2].
- **Union Suppression**: Massive expenditures are being directed toward anti-union efforts, with Amazon alone spending over $26 million on labor consultants in 2025 to defeat organizing campaigns [1].
- **Wage Standards**: Legislators are actively pursuing "sub-minimum" or tiered wage structures, such as Missouri's SB 1325, which aims to establish a lower minimum wage specifically for workers under 18 [3].
- **Workplace Safety Deregulation**: The U.S. Department of Labor is pursuing "ambitious" rollbacks of safety standards, including proposals to eliminate lighting requirements at construction sites and limit the enforcement power of OSHA's general duty clause [4].

### Findings

#### Gig Economy and Benefit Structures
- **Portable Benefits as a Replacement**: Proponents suggest portable benefits—programs tied to the individual rather than an employer—can solve the needs of the 30 million Americans working independently [5]. However, labor advocates argue these are "gimmicks" or "glorified savings accounts" designed to allow corporations to bypass providing standard employment rights and benefits [8].
- **Classification Battles**: The legal distinction between "employee" and "independent contractor" remains a central conflict, with the DOL attempting to clarify rules to prevent misclassification under the Fair Labor Standards Act [1].

#### Union Busting and Labor Organizing
- **Corporate Spending on Consultants**: Amazon reported spending more than $26 million on anti-union labor consultants in 2025, a record-high expenditure [1].
- **Targeted Campaigns**: The spending was directed at firms specializing in defeating union organizing, with Amazon issuing hundreds of payments averaging over $56,000 each—a figure exceeding the average annual wage of some of its warehouse workers [1]. These efforts have targeted fulfillment centers, distribution hubs, and subcontracted driver networks [1].

#### Wage Standards and Youth Employment
- **Sub-Minimum Wage Legislation**: In Missouri, legislation (SB 1325) has been proposed to create a "youth minimum wage" of $12.30 per hour, significantly lower than the $15.00 statewide rate for adults [3].
- **Economic Arguments for Tiers**: Supporters of lower youth wages argue these roles are "entry-level" requiring high supervision and that lower costs help small businesses manage rising labor expenses and combat automation [3]. 
- **Impact on Families**: Opponents, including the Missouri AFL-CIO, argue this creates a "two-tier wage system" that penalizes young workers and families who depend on teen income to offset rising costs like gas and food [3].

#### Workplace Safety and Deregulation
- **Rollback of Safety Standards**: The U.S. Department of Labor has proposed eliminating over 60 "obsolete" regulations, including a requirement for adequate lighting at construction sites [4].
- **Limiting Enforcement Power**: New proposals seek to limit OSHA’s "general duty clause" authority, which allows the agency to penalize employers for unsafe conditions not covered by specific standards [4]. 
- **Industry Justification**: The administration frames these moves as "slashing red tape" to "stifle growth" and "limit opportunity," arguing that reducing compliance costs will help businesses expand [4].
