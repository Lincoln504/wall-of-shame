## Normalizing Excessive State Power and the Exploitation of Carceral Systems

### Executive Summary

This investigation examines the mechanisms by which excessive state power is defended and exploitative carceral systems are normalized in the United States. Across six key strategies — violence justification through "split-second" rationales, legal shielding via qualified immunity, carceral profiteering through prison labor, rhetorical attacks on criminal justice reform, "law and order" rhetoric driving mass incarceration, and expansion of police surveillance — a consistent pattern emerges: legal doctrines, economic incentives, and political rhetoric work in concert to insulate the carceral state from accountability while making its excesses seem inevitable or justified. Key findings include the Supreme Court's foundational role in establishing the "split-second" standard for police use of force in *Graham v. Connor* (1989); the admission by Nixon domestic policy chief John Ehrlichman that the War on Drugs was designed to criminalize Black people and disrupt antiwar communities; an AP investigation tracing nearly $200 million in prison agricultural sales to major food brands like McDonald's, Walmart, and Cargill; data showing appellate courts granted qualified immunity in 57% of excessive force cases (2017-2019), up from 44% (2005-2007); and the finding that 4.6 million Americans remain barred from voting due to felony convictions.

### Findings

#### I. VIOLENCE JUSTIFICATION: The "Split-Second" Doctrine and Its Judicial Codification

- **The *Graham v. Connor* standard established the "split-second" framework**: The U.S. Supreme Court's 1989 decision in *Graham v. Connor* (490 U.S. 386) set the foundational standard for evaluating police use of force under the Fourth Amendment. The Court held that "the 'reasonableness' of a particular use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, and its calculus must embody an allowance for the fact that police officers are often forced to make split-second decisions about the amount of force necessary in a particular situation."[7] This language has been cited by courts and police advocacy organizations for decades to justify expansive use of force.

- **Legal training materials reinforce the "split-second" framing**: The Police Organization Providing Peer Assistance (POPA) and Legal Defense Fund blog explicitly advises that "Reasonableness takes into consideration that police often make split-second decisions in tense, uncertain, dangerous and rapidly developing situations — situations where officers act quickly and without all the facts." The material adds that "Judges realize it is not fair to Monday-morning-quarterback a cop's use of force from the comfort and safety of the judge..."[3] This framing normalizes excessive force by presenting any after-the-fact scrutiny as inherently unfair.

- **The standard is applied broadly even in extreme cases**: In *Napouk v. Las Vegas Metro Police Department*, the Ninth Circuit applied the split-second framework to the fatal shooting of Lloyd Napouk, "highlighting the challenges law enforcement officers face when making split-second decisions in tense, uncertain, and rapidly evolving situations."[5] An analysis in the Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law noted that "Humans are prone to err, and police officers are challenged by having to perform split-second threat assessment with the potential of deadly intervention," adding that the dissent in that case "was concerned about sending a message of absolute immunity to law enforcement."[6]

- **The "split-second" excuse extends to using suspects as human shields**: Lexipol, a major provider of police policy and training content, covered a case where "the court upholds the grant of qualified immunity in light of the fact that the officer's split-second decisions were not deemed unlawful" — in a case involving an officer who used a suspect as a human shield during a gunfight.[8]

#### II. AVOIDING ACCOUNTABILITY: Qualified Immunity as a Doctrinal Shield

- **Qualified immunity is a judicially created doctrine, not a statute**: According to the Congressional Research Service (CRS), qualified immunity is "a judicially created doctrine shielding from civil liability those public officials who perform discretionary functions." It was developed by the Supreme Court as part of its interpretation of the Civil Rights Act of 1871 (the Ku Klux Klan Act). The CRS report states that "The Supreme Court has observed that qualified immunity balances two important interests — 'the need to hold public officials accountable when they exercise power irresponsibly and the need to shield officials from harassment, distraction, and liability when they perform their duties reasonably.'" The immunity's broad protection is intended for "all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law" and to give government officials "breathing room" to make reasonable mistakes of fact and law.[1]

- **The doctrine has dramatically expanded over time**: The CRS report documents that the Supreme Court heard 18 qualified immunity cases from 2000 to 2016, and in 16 of those cases — many involving allegations of excessive force — the Court found officials were entitled to immunity because the law was not "clearly established." The Court has increasingly required that the "clearly established" right be defined with "specificity," such that even minor differences between cases can immunize officers.[1] In *City of Escondido v. Emmons* (2019), the Court held that the appropriate inquiry is not whether the officer violated a general right to be free from excessive force, but whether clearly established law "prohibited the officers from stopping and taking down a man in these circumstances."[1]

- **Statistics show qualified immunity is increasingly granted**: The CRS report cites a study finding that appellate courts showed an "increasing tendency to grant qualified immunity, particularly in excessive force cases." From 2005 to 2007, 44% of courts favored police in excessive force cases. That number jumped to 57% in excessive force cases decided from 2017 to 2019.[1]

- **Justice Sotomayor has argued qualified immunity has become an "absolute shield"**: The CRS report notes that Justice Sonia Sotomayor, dissenting in several qualified immunity cases, "expressed her disfavor with the modern approach, fearing its application essentially provides an absolute shield for law enforcement officers and 'renders the protections of the Fourth Amendment hollow.'"[1]

- **Defenders argue the doctrine is necessary**: The CRS report notes that defenders of qualified immunity say it "plays an important role in affording police officers some level of deference when making split-second decisions." Some members of law enforcement find it "comforting" to know the doctrine protects all but "the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law."[1]

- **Reform efforts have been proposed in Congress**: The CRS outlines several legislative proposals, including the Ending Qualified Immunity Act and the Justice in Policing Act of 2020 and 2021, which would have amended Section 1983 to abolish the qualified immunity defense. An alternative approach in the Reforming Qualified Immunity Act would have shifted the burden to Section 1983 defendants to show the law authorized their conduct, rather than requiring plaintiffs to show clearly established law prohibited it.[1]

- **The ABA has documented qualified immunity's effect on police misconduct**: The American Bar Association reports that Colorado, as part of its Law Enforcement Integrity and Accountability Act, "created a civil action against law enforcement officers who violate people's constitutional rights, and expressly provided that 'qualified immunity is not a defense to liability.'"[1]

- **Indemnification undermines the rationale for qualified immunity**: The CRS report notes that former Justice Stephen Breyer argued that indemnification by police departments may alleviate concerns about facing liability. According to one study cited in the report, police officers are "virtually always indemnified," meaning even when found liable, the city or county covers damages.[1]

- **Scholarly analysis calls qualified immunity an "unlawful shield"**: An analysis in the journal *Race and Justice* described qualified immunity as a doctrine that "has silently flourished throughout America," created to "shield police officers and other government officials for the actions they engage on the job." The piece connects the doctrine to national movements for police accountability following the use of excessive force against unarmed civilians.[6]

#### III. CARCERAL PROFIT: Prison Labor as a Multi-Billion Dollar Enterprise

- **The 13th Amendment's "exception clause" provides constitutional cover for forced prison labor**: The 13th Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude "except as punishment for a crime." Walk Free, a human rights organization, reports that this loophole fuels forced labor in U.S. prisons, with "the government and private companies profiting. This system disproportionately impacts people of colour and keeps them in cycles of poverty and exploitation."[2]

- **Incarcerated workers earn pennies per hour**: The Sentencing Project's "One in Five" report, citing an ACLU and University of Chicago Law School study, finds that "the average minimum wage for the most common forms of prison labor is $0.13/hour, and the average maximum is $0.52/hour." Seven states — Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, and Texas — do not compensate incarcerated people at all for the vast majority of jobs.[9]

- **Nearly three-quarters of incarcerated workers report their work is required**: According to the Sentencing Project, "of the roughly 60% of incarcerated people who reported having a work assignment in prison, nearly three-quarters reported their work assignments were required." People subject to this forced labor are often unprotected from basic workplace health and safety standards such as those provided by OSHA, "leading to sometimes dangerous and inhumane working conditions."[9]

- **An AP investigation traced nearly $200 million in prison agricultural sales**: The two-year Associated Press investigation tracked prison labor entering the supply chains of "some of the world's largest food companies and most popular brands." The AP found goods from Frosted Flakes cereal and Ball Park hot dogs to Gold Medal flour, Coca-Cola, and Riceland rice were linked to prison labor. The investigation traced nearly $200 million in sales of farmed goods and livestock to businesses over six years. Inmates working at the Louisiana State Penitentiary (Angola), a former slave plantation, "can earn between 2 cents and 40 cents an hour" and work in fields overseen by armed guards on horseback.[4]

- **Major corporations buy directly from prisons**: The AP found that Cargill, Bunge, Louis Dreyfus, Archer Daniels Midland, and Consolidated Grain and Barge — which together post annual revenues of more than $400 billion — have in recent years "scooped up millions of dollars' worth of soy, corn and wheat straight from prisons, which compete with local farmers."[4]

- **Prisoners face dangerous conditions with few protections**: The AP documented cases of incarcerated workers being killed on the job. Frank Dwayne Ellington, serving life in Alabama under a habitual offenders act, was killed in 2017 at a Koch Foods poultry plant when a machine "sucked him inside, crushing his skull." Koch Foods initially argued Ellington wasn't technically an employee. OSHA fined the company $19,500.[4]

- **Hickman's Family Farms provides a case study in carceral profiteering**: The AP reported that Hickman's, the Southwest's largest egg producer, has employed prisoners for nearly 30 years. It is the Arizona corrections department's largest labor contractor, bringing in nearly $35 million in revenue over six fiscal years. During COVID-19, about 140 women were moved from their prison to a metal hangar on Hickman's property, yet still had 30% of their wages deducted by the state for "room and board" despite living in a makeshift dormitory. Workers earned less than $3 per hour after deductions.[4]

- **Historical roots in convict leasing**: The AP notes that "the convict-leasing period, which officially ended in 1928, helped chart the path to America's modern-day prison-industrial complex." After the Civil War, the 13th Amendment's exception clause "provided legal cover to round up thousands of mostly young Black men" who were jailed for petty offenses and "leased out by states to plantations like Angola and some of the country's biggest companies, including coal mines and railroads."[4]

- **Prison labor produces over $11 billion in goods annually**: The Sentencing Project reports incarcerated workers produce goods and services "amounting to over $11 billion every year, yet they remain unfairly compensated for their labor, with average nominal wages even lower today than they were in 2001."[9] A partial list of companies that have used prison labor includes Allstate, Best Western, Dell Computer, Eddie Bauer, McDonald's, Starbucks, Nike, and Victoria's Secret.[3]

- **Corporate profiteering extends beyond private prisons**: Prism Reports notes that "conversations about profiteering within the criminal legal system have centered predominantly on private prisons and prison labor, ignoring the sprawling, insidious network of financial incentives and private corporations that shape incarceration and detention in the U.S." Worth Rises has released a Prison Industry Corporate Database tracking "more than 4,000 corporations that profit from the carceral system."[3]

- **Efforts to abolish forced prison labor face implementation gaps**: Colorado became the first state to remove language allowing slavery as punishment for a crime from its constitution in 2018. However, the Sentencing Project notes that a class action lawsuit filed in Colorado in 2022 "highlights that incarcerated individuals there continue to be forced to work under threats of punishment including losing family contact privileges and being moved into solitary confinement."[9]

#### IV. ATTACKING REFORM: Blaming Criminal Justice Reform for Crime

- **Opponents of bail reform claim it threatens public safety**: The Prison Policy Initiative's 2023-2024 annual report documents that "opponents — including district attorneys, police departments, and the commercial bail industry — often claim pretrial reform puts community safety at risk." These claims persist despite empirical evidence to the contrary.[2]

- **Multiple studies find no evidence that bail reform causes crime waves**: The American Prospect reported on a growing body of empirical work showing "no evidence at all to support claims by conservatives and moderates that criminal justice reform, particularly bail reform, causes crime waves." Studies by "academic researchers, advocates, and even some court systems themselves show the same results." The article notes that "findings add to a growing body of empirical work" on this topic.[6]

- **A House Judiciary Committee report explicitly blamed reform for violent crime**: In October 2024, the House Judiciary Committee (then under Republican control) released a report titled "Violent Crime in America: How the Pro-criminal, Anti-law Enforcement Policies of the Progressive Left Are Endangering Communities." The report alleged that "Democrat-run state legislatures, city councils, and rogue prosecutors implemented far-left bail reform laws and other policies that worsened rising levels of violent crime."[7]

- **"Tough-on-crime" policies are returning in places that had embraced reform**: Stateline reported that "a notable shift in criminal justice policy is happening, as some states embrace 'tough-on-crime' policies, including stiffer penalties and expanded police powers, in a marked departure from recent years." This shift comes despite data showing that states that reduced incarceration also saw crime reductions.[5]

- **FWD.us found crime fell faster in states that reduced imprisonment**: A 2024 report from FWD.us found that "from 2012-2022, 45 states saw reductions in crime rates, while imprisoning fewer people, with crime falling faster in states that reduced imprisonment than in states that increased it."[10]

- **California's anti-reform campaigns exploit public anger**: The Center on Juvenile and Criminal Justice reported that "the anti-reform campaign is also exploiting public anger that retail thieves are 'getting away with crime,' while the media fails to hold law enforcement and conservative jurisdictions accountable for their own failed practices." The report notes that California's criminal justice statistics "clearly show" certain trends that are being misrepresented.[9]

#### V. HARSHER SENTENCING: "Law and Order" Rhetoric and Mass Incarceration

- **Nixon adviser admitted the War on Drugs was designed to target Black people**: The Equal Justice Initiative (EJI) reports that in a 1994 interview, President Nixon's domestic policy chief John Ehrlichman stated: "The Nixon campaign in 1968, and the Nixon White House after that, had two enemies: the antiwar left and Black people. You understand what I'm saying? We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or Black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities. We could arrest their leaders, raid their homes, break up their meetings, and vilify them night after night on the evening news. Did we know we were lying about the drugs? Of course we did."[8]

- **The War on Drugs drove mass incarceration with racialized impact**: The EJI reports that after Nixon declared the War on Drugs in 1971, incarceration escalated from 300,000 to 2.3 million. Half of those in federal prison are incarcerated for a drug offense, and two-thirds of those in prison for drug offenses are people of color. Between 1980 and 2011, arrests of African Americans for violent and property crimes fell, but "rose dramatically for drug offenses." African Americans are far more likely to be arrested for drug offenses than whites, even though "whites use drugs at the same rate and are more likely to sell drugs."[8]

- **The 1994 Crime Bill amplified mass incarceration**: The EJI notes that President Bill Clinton's 1994 Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act allotted $12.5 billion to states to increase incarceration. The Center for American Progress reports that the 1994 Crime Bill had three continuing harms on communities of color: it incentivized mass incarceration through truth-in-sentencing grants, expanded the death penalty to cover more than 60 federal crimes, and created a federal "three strikes" mandatory life imprisonment provision.[8]

- **Mandatory minimums and three-strikes laws perpetuate mass incarceration**: The Sentencing Project reports that mandatory prison sentences for drug crimes, longer mandatory minimums for violent and repeat offenses, and three-strikes laws requiring minimum sentences of 25 years to life were key drivers of mass incarceration. These policies "lengthened sentences, requiring minimum sentences of 25 years to life imprisonment for some, usually serious, offenses."[9]

- **Iowa passed a new three-strikes law in 2026**: The USA TODAY Network reported that in May 2026, "Iowa lawmakers approve steeper sentences for repeat felony offenders" under a new three-strikes law. The Iowa Department of Corrections "consistently reports operating above its capacity for inmates." This comes as "some states, and the federal government, have rolled back their own harsher sentencing for repeat offenders," with the article characterizing Iowa as "'zagging' against national trend."[2]

- **Mass incarceration deepened inequality**: The Sentencing Project's research found that "mass incarceration increased the U.S. poverty rate by at least 20% between 1980 and 2004." Black Americans remain imprisoned at nearly five times the rate of whites. One in three Black adult men (33%) had a felony conviction on their record in 2010, compared to 13% of all adult men. Black children are far more likely than white children to experience parental incarceration, which leads to "psychological stress, antisocial behavior, academic suspension or expulsion, economic hardship, and criminal activity."[9]

#### VI. EXPANDING CONTROL: Surveillance and Police Technology

- **Facial recognition technology is being expanded despite ethical concerns**: Forbes reported that "facial recognition can be part of building smarter, safer and more equitable communities, without sacrificing our collective values" when proper safeguards are in place — framing that normalizes the expansion of biometric surveillance technology.[3]

- **AI-equipped police body cameras are being deployed in real-time**: The Associated Press reported that in Edmonton, Canada, "police body cameras equipped with artificial intelligence have been trained to detect the faces of about 7,000 people on a 'high risk' watch list." Axon Enterprise, the leading body camera manufacturer, has been central to this deployment, and the cameras represent "a live test of whether facial recognition technology shunned as too intrusive could have a place in policing throughout North America."[4]

- **The UK is actively expanding police facial recognition use**: The BBC reported that the Home Office "hopes a new public consultation will lead to new laws expanding the use of the technology by more police forces."[9]

- **Body cameras are premised on the assumption that surveillance deterrence works**: R Street's research on body cameras notes that "the core premise behind body cameras rests on the assumption that people behave better when they think they are being watched. The theory was this sense of perceived 'social surveillance' would provide everyone with an incentive to behave—officers and civilians alike." The line between "public security and state surveillance lies not in technology, but in the policies that govern it."[5]

- **Real-time surveillance networks are expanding**: LVT (a surveillance technology company) describes a "real-time revolution in police surveillance" where "some cameras can also be equipped with facial recognition and ALPRs" and "many law enforcement agencies offer programs that invite residents and private business owners to register their private camera networks so that officers can request footage in the case of future crimes."[7]

- **Public opinion on facial recognition is divided**: A Pew Research Center survey found that the public is "more likely to see facial recognition use by police as good rather than bad for society," but a majority opposes facial recognition use by social media sites. About half favor its use by retail stores and apartment buildings.[10]

#### VII. INTERSECTION: How These Strategies Work Together

- **The prison-industrial complex represents a deeply entrenched system**: The US Resist News economic policy brief describes the prison-industrial complex as "a deeply entrenched system where incarceration is commodified, intertwining the interests of government agencies and private enterprises. This nexus has transformed the U.S. criminal justice system into a profit-driven enterprise, often at the expense of marginalized communities."[9]

- **Profit incentives are deeply enmeshed with mass incarceration**: The Wisconsin Law Review's analysis of "Bloody Lucre: Carceral Labor and Prison Profit" notes that "removing the cash from carceral labor will be no easy task given the deep enmeshing of profitability with mass incarceration. The economic forces underlying and benefiting from carceral labor range from investment funds to major corporations and are thus difficult to dislodge from the prison-industrial complex."[5]

- **Scaling back criminal legal contact improves public safety**: The Sentencing Project's "One in Five" report finds that numerous studies demonstrate that police contact during adolescence increases future delinquency and violence — a 2020 study found the odds of committing delinquent or violent acts were 44% to 81% higher for youth who had prior police contact. Declining to charge non-violent misdemeanors reduces future offending. Additionally, "short terms of imprisonment in carceral settings with limited rehabilitative programs result in higher recidivism rates than community supervision."[9]
