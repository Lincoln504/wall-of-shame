## The Defense of State Violence and Normalization of Exploitative Carceral Systems

### Executive Summary

This report examines the legal doctrines, legislative strategies, rhetorical frames, and political backlash that defend state violence and normalize exploitative carceral systems in the United States. Drawing on Supreme Court decisions from 2025-2026, model legislation from the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), state-level legislative battles, and analysis of the post-George Floyd reform landscape, the report documents five interrelated dimensions: (1) **brutality apologia** through the "split-second decision" and "reasonableness" frames that shield officers; (2) **qualified immunity** as a judicially created doctrine that blocks accountability, recently expanded in the 2026 *Zorn v. Linton* and *Smith v. Scott* cases; (3) **prison labor** exploited through ALEC's Prison Industries Act and the 13th Amendment loophole, with inmates paid as little as $0.13-$0.52/hour on average; (4) **accountability opposition** as civilian oversight boards are stripped of power and consent decrees terminated; and (5) **mass incarceration** defended through "tough on crime" backlash, opposition to bail reform, and pathologizing over-policed communities.

#### I. QUALIFIED IMMUNITY: Judicial Doctrine as Shield

**The Zorn v. Linton Expansion (March 2026)**

In March 2026, the Supreme Court decided *Zorn v. Linton*, significantly expanding qualified immunity protections for police officers. The case involved Vermont police Sergeant Jacob Zorn, who held a peaceful protestor named Shela Linton in a rear wristlock — a "pain compliance" measure specifically designed to cause pain until submission, which Vermont's own police guidelines said should not be used on passive protestors. Linton suffered physical and psychological injuries including permanent loss of movement [2].

The 2nd U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals had sided with Linton, finding that a jury could conclude Zorn violated the Constitution and that he violated a "clearly established" rule from a previous case. But the Supreme Court reversed in a 6-3 decision, holding that the officer should be protected by qualified immunity. The Court drew an exceptionally fine distinction: even though there was a prior case finding a wristlock could constitute excessive force, the Supreme Court emphasized that the previous case involved no warnings prior to the cop's use of force. Because Zorn allegedly issued a "warning" before using the pain compliance measure, the Court ruled it was not the same situation, and the officer was protected from civil liability [2].

The Slate analysis calls this reasoning "callous and illogical," noting that the legal question under the Fourth Amendment is whether the officer used greater force than needed — not whether the force was announced. The Court's new standard "suggests a chilling legal sanctioning of officer threats," potentially making threats like "Get out of the car or I'm going to fucking smoke you" more legally defensible if characterized as a "warning" [2].

**Smith v. Scott: Summary Disposition Without Argument (April 2026)**

On April 20, 2026, the Supreme Court used an unsigned one-paragraph order — a "shadow docket" summary disposition — to vacate a 9th Circuit ruling that would have provided justice to the family of Roy Scott, a 65-year-old Black man from Las Vegas killed by police [2].

Roy Scott had called the police for medical help, telling officers he had paranoid schizophrenia and was having active hallucinations. In subduing him, two officers held him down for several minutes, forcing his arms down and holding him on his back. The officers then flipped Scott face down and placed their bodyweight on his back and neck for another couple of minutes. By the time the cops got off him, he was unresponsive. Soon after, he was declared dead. Scott was unarmed, not suspected of any crime, and in the middle of a mental health episode when he was killed [2].

The 9th Circuit had sided with Scott's family, holding that a jury could reasonably find the officers used excessive force. The Supreme Court vacated this decision 6-3, without full briefing or oral argument, citing *Zorn v. Linton* as justification. The majority did not justify its decision in a written opinion. The Slate article describes the result as "as weak as it is violent" [2].

**The Qualified Immunity Act of 2025**

On January 16, 2025, the Qualified Immunity Act of 2025 (S.122) was introduced in the U.S. Senate. According to the bill text, it "provides statutory authority for qualified immunity for law enforcement officers in civil cases involving constitutional violations." The bill aims to codify the judicially created doctrine into statutory law. The House version (H.R. 503) states that "qualified immunity balances two important interests — the need to hold law enforcement officers accountable when they exercise power irresponsibly and the need to shield officers from harassment, distraction, and liability when they perform their duties reasonably" [1][6].

**Barnes v. Felix (May 2025): The "Moment-of-Threat" Rule Rejected**

In a unanimous decision on May 15, 2025, the Supreme Court rejected the Fifth Circuit's "moment-of-threat" rule in *Barnes v. Felix*. The case involved Roberto Felix Jr., a law enforcement officer who fatally shot Ashtian Barnes during a traffic stop. The lower courts had applied a rule that limited analysis to the "two seconds" when the officer was in danger, excluding any consideration of events leading up to the shooting — including the officer's own decision to jump onto the car. Justice Kagan wrote that this approach "cannot review the totality of the circumstances if it has put on chronological blinders" [4][12].

However, the concurrence by Justice Kavanaugh — joined by Thomas, Alito, and Barrett — demonstrates the durability of the "split-second decision" defense. Kavanaugh's opinion extensively detailed the dangers officers face during traffic stops, citing cases of officers killed on duty and listing "dangerous" options available to officers. He wrote that when a driver "abruptly pulls away during a traffic stop, an officer has no particularly good or safe options," and that "judges should keep in mind that it is one thing to dissect and scrutinize an officer's actions with the '20/20 vision of hindsight' ... It is quite another to make 'split-second judgments' on the ground, in 'circumstances that are tense, uncertain, and rapidly evolving'" [4].

Bowling Green State University criminologist Philip Stinson predicted the *Barnes* decision "is going to, in many respects, quite often benefit the police" because in a legal climate where juries and judges are hesitant to second-guess police, allowing them to introduce more context could make convictions harder to obtain [12].

**Alabama "Back the Blue" Law (May 2025)**

In May 2025, Alabama lawmakers passed the "Back the Blue" law, expanding legal immunity for police officers. This occurred just a few years after an onslaught of thousands of proposed state bills aimed at increasing police accountability following George Floyd's murder [12].

#### III. PRISON LABOR: ALEC Model Legislation and the 13th Amendment Loophole

**The Constitutional Framework**

The 13th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution abolished slavery and involuntary servitude "except as a punishment for crime." This "Except Clause" has long been used to justify forced labor in federal and state prison systems. The loophole fuels a system where incarcerated people are forced to work for little or no pay, often under threat of punishment, while the state and private companies benefit [3][8].

**ALEC's Prison Industries Act**

The American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) has been instrumental in expanding prison labor for private industry. ALEC's Prison Industries Act — adopted by ALEC's Criminal Justice Task Force on May 1, 2004 and amended July 29, 2004 — "provides for the employment of inmate labor in state correctional institutions and in the private manufacturing of certain products under specific conditions." Its stated purpose is "to reduce recidivism and reduce state prison costs" [5].

Key provisions of the ALEC model legislation include:

- **Private Sector Contracts**: The division "may enter into a contract with a private business to conduct a program on or off property operated by the department" [5].

- **Lease of Land**: Prisons may "lease prison land to a private business" for up to 50 years. The business "must lease the land at a mutually agreed upon price and may construct or convert plant facilities on the land" [5].

- **PIECP Wage System**: The model bill requires that participants be paid not less than the "prison industry enhancement certification program (PIECP) wage," which is defined as "the wage paid by the employer for work of a similar nature in the location in which the work is performed." However, the authority "may permit employers to pay a participant the federal minimum wage for the two month period beginning on the date participation begins," and wages for youth commission participants are capped at the federal minimum wage [5].

- **Deduction System**: The model bill creates a "Private Sector Prison Industries Expansion Account," funded by deductions from participants' wages, to "construct work facilities, recruit corporations to participate as private sector industries programs" [5].

- **Private Sector Prison Industries Oversight Authority**: An eight-member authority appointed by the governor, including representatives of organized labor, employers, victims' rights groups, inmates' rights groups, vocational rehabilitation, and three public members [5].

**The History of ALEC's Prison Labor Expansion**

The Nation's investigation into ALEC revealed that the organization helped pioneer tough sentencing laws like mandatory minimums, "three strikes" laws, and "truth in sentencing" laws — in 1995 alone, ALEC's Truth in Sentencing Act was signed into law in 25 states (then-State Rep. Scott Walker was an ALEC member who sponsored Wisconsin's truth-in-sentencing laws). ALEC then proposed "solutions" to the overcrowding it helped create, such as privatizing the parole process, creating private for-profit prisons (benefiting CCA and GEO Group, two major ALEC corporate sponsors), and expanding prison labor [6].

The Prison Industries Act was first crafted in 1993 by Texas State Representative and ALEC member Ray Allen, who authored the Texas Prison Industries Act. After it passed in Texas, Allen advocated for it to be duplicated nationwide. Allen later became Chairman of ALEC's Criminal Justice Task Force. He resigned from the state legislature in 2006 while under investigation for unethical lobbying practices and was hired soon after as a lobbyist for GEO Group [6].

As of 2010, there were "thirty jurisdictions with active [PIE] operations" including Arizona, Arkansas, California, Colorado, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, and twelve more. States including Kentucky, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and New Hampshire were in the process of joining [6].

**Current Wage Exploitation**

The average minimum hourly wage paid to incarcerated workers for non-industry jobs is 13 cents, and the average maximum hourly wage is 52 cents, according to ACLU research. State-owned prison industries pay 30 cents to $1.30 per hour on average [8][10]. The overwhelming majority of workers have jobs that support prison facility operations and are compensated at the lower end of these pay scales [8].

For example, Oregon's DMV center pays its incarcerated workers $4 to $6 a day. The federal Prison Industries Enhancement Certification Program (PIE) was designed "to encourage states and units of local government to establish employment opportunities for prisoners that approximate private sector work opportunities," but in practice allows wages far below market rate [6][10].

**California's 2025 Legislative Battle**

In California, current law (Penal Code § 4019.3) sets a maximum wage of $2.00 per eight-hour shift for incarcerated workers in county jails — 25 cents per hour. Assemblymember Isaac Bryan introduced AB 248 to repeal this maximum wage, calling it "a relic of slavery" [11].

Bryan explained: "At that rate, combined with predatory commissary prices, it would take you a full business week to be able to afford a two-ounce tube of toothpaste and a bag of hot Cheetos." Despite working full time — often overtime, seven days a week — many prisoners continue to carry the debt of court-mandated victims' restitution, unable to pay because of near-zero wages [11].

The California State Sheriffs' Association opposed the bill, arguing it "would create pressure on counties to increase the fiscal recompense provided to inmate workers, thereby potentially limiting the availability of opportunities for work while incarcerated" [11].

Republican Assemblymember Heather Hadwick opposed the bill, stating: "This bill ignores the fundamental purpose of inmate labor programs. They're not traditional employment. They're part of a rehabilitation process that provides structure, skill building and a sense of responsibility" [11].

Assemblymember Carl DeMaio argued: "When people are in jail, when they're in prison, there's a substantial cost to taxpayers. It's not unreasonable to expect inmates to help chip in to do a little bit of the work" [11].

The California Supreme Court ruled in *Ruelas vs. Alameda County* (April 2024) that county jail inmate workers — even those not ultimately convicted — are not entitled to state minimum wage requirements, defining jail work compensation as adequate in terms of offering sentence work credit, rehabilitative value, and job-skills training [11].

Separately, AB 247 would impose $19/hour pay for incarcerated firefighters during active fire incidents. Despite voters rejecting Proposition 6 in November 2024 — which would have amended the state constitution to abolish involuntary prison labor — lawmakers are pushing forward [11].

**Minnesota Prison Labor Reform**

A 2025 audit showed that MinnCor, Minnesota's prison industry program, underpaid incarcerated workers while subsidizing corporations. Advocates are pushing for fair wages, union rights, and worker protections [10].

**Corporate Profiting from Prison Labor**

Using a leaked document trove of 800 model bills, researchers have analyzed ALEC's "hidden corporate profit making in the prison-industrial complex," finding that ALEC seeks to expand the private prison industry in three ways: (1) promoting greater use of private prisons, goods, and services; (2) promoting greater use of prison labor; and (3) expanding the pool of people subject to incarceration [7]. The two largest private-prison corporations, CoreCivic and GEO Group, currently face five federal class action lawsuits for allegedly violating the Trafficking Victims Protection Act's forced labor provision [7].

#### IV. ACCOUNTABILITY OPPOSITION: Attacking Civilian Oversight, Framing Reform as the Cause of Crime

**Rollback of Civilian Oversight (2025)**

Five years after George Floyd's murder, police reforms are facing coordinated rollback. The Marshall Project documented multiple examples from the week of May 17, 2025 alone [12]:

- **Rochester, New York**: The State Supreme Court stripped the Rochester Police Accountability Board of its investigatory power. The body already lacked disciplinary authority, meaning according to WXXI News, "the only thing it can do now is review and recommend changes to department policy" [12].

- **Baltimore County, Maryland**: The county's Police Accountability Board — created in 2022 as part of a statewide reform push — is "in the midst of an identity crisis" over whether board members are even legally entitled to read citizen complaints, let alone act on them [12].

- **Champaign, Illinois**: A citizen review subcommittee operates with "similarly narrow authority, lacking the power to initiate its own inquiries or enforce disciplinary outcomes." Activist Sundiata Cha-Jua told Illinois Public Media: "They take the name and the kinds of things we've argued for, and they say that they're going to implement it, they slow walk it, and then when they do, it has none of the power that we were advocating for" [12].

**Termination of Federal Consent Decrees**

The Trump administration has moved to terminate multiple existing consent decrees and generally take the Department of Justice out of the business of guiding police reform. This has thrown the future of oversight agreements into doubt in multiple cities [12]:

- **New Orleans**: A "sudden exodus" of DOJ attorneys has thrown the future of the city's decade-long oversight agreement into doubt, just as it entered its "sustainment period" on the path to ending federal oversight [12].

- **Minneapolis**: Initial negotiations on a consent decree announced nearly a year ago are stalled, and some local observers expect the Trump administration to withdraw before a formal agreement can be reached [12].

- **Federal Funding Cuts**: The administration terminated about half a billion dollars in grant funding, mostly to nonprofits and local governments for programs targeting violent crime, domestic violence, and civilian co-responder teams. Meanwhile, the DOJ's COPS office announced $156 million in funding for agencies to hire new or rehire existing law enforcement officers [12].

**Police Union Contracts as Accountability Barriers**

Police union contracts across the country have been documented to shield officers from discipline and scrutiny. Key patterns include contracts that "erase disciplinary records or allow police to forfeit sick leave for suspensions" while residents face hurdles in pursuing complaints [12].

In **Akron, Ohio** (April 2026), the city updated its police union contract on discipline but left out civilian oversight issues. A memorandum of understanding signed March 13, 2026, established 110-day deadlines for disciplinary investigations, including in deadly use of force investigations. The head of the Citizens Police Oversight Board expressed hope this could open the door for bigger changes — but civilian oversight was not included [12].

In **Seattle** (March 2026), the new police contract — running through 2027 — "awards big raises to officers, but doesn't implement several long-promised reforms intended to boost civilian oversight and ensure timely police discipline" [12].

Research analyzing 178 police union contracts found provisions that "limit officer interrogations after alleged misconduct, mandate the destruction of disciplinary records, ban civilian oversight, and subject disciplinary actions to arbitration that in nearly all cases overturns discipline" [12].

**Police Union Defense of Qualified Immunity**

Defenders of qualified immunity argue the doctrine "provides breathing room for public officials, particularly law enforcement officers to do their jobs effectively." Another argument raised in defense is that "the threat of massive personal liability for good-faith mistakes will deter people from becoming police officers" [12].

The National Police Association filed an amicus brief in the Supreme Court in May 2025 urging the Court to prevent liability, which aligned with the outcome in a case where the Court "reaffirmed that officers are entitled to qualified immunity unless their conduct violates clearly established law defined with specificity. Broad or generalized precedent cannot be used to impose liability" [1].

#### V. MASS INCARCERATION AND REGRESSIVE SENTENCING

**Reform Backlash and the "Crime Wave" Frame**

The Marshall Project notes that "tracking rollbacks and backlash from the 2020 highwater mark for justice reform enthusiasm has been a recurring theme." Multiple indicators show a national retreat from the political momentum that police and justice reform efforts held in 2020 [12].

The Minnesota Freedom Fund — the bail fund that gained national prominence during the George Floyd protests — is "stepping back from its once core mission of paying bail" [12].

The "defund the police" movement saw cities quickly reverse course due to "rising concerns about crime, political incentives to maintain funding for police departments, and public pressure to prioritize public safety." A 2025 scientific study documented that changes to defund police were "largely short-lived" [12].

**Pathologizing Over-Policed Communities**

The Sentencing Project's Toolkit for Fighting Mass Incarceration in the 119th Congress (March 2025) documents the ongoing struggle against regressive federal sentencing policies. The Sentencing Project warns that a measure that passed is "set to undo a decade of progress towards unraveling mass incarceration without any public safety benefit" and is projected to "increase the state's prison population by 35% in the next five years" [12].

Mass incarceration was described as "a threat to public health and safety" by the Justice Education Project in 2025, noting that "mass incarceration doesn't just harm nearly 2 million convicts, but it creates rippling effects damaging communities, families, and even our own well-being." The Vera Institute reveals that each year spent in prisons leads to a two-year decline in life expectancy [12].

**ALEC's Role in Mass Incarceration**

ALEC's broader legislative agenda contributed directly to the explosion of the U.S. prison population. The organization helped pioneer mandatory minimums for non-violent drug offenders, "three strikes" laws, and "truth in sentencing" laws. ALEC also worked to pass state laws to create private for-profit prisons, benefiting Corrections Corporation of America (CCA) and Geo Group, and arranged secret meetings between Arizona state legislators and CCA to draft SB 1070, Arizona's notorious immigration law, "to keep CCA prisons flush with immigrant detainees" [6].

#### VI. PRISON ABOLITION: The "Dangerous People" Reframe

Arguments defending the carceral system frequently invoke the question: "What about the dangerous people? Who would keep us safe?" Prison abolition is framed as an "impossible idea" in U.S. public discourse where "incarceration has become a normalized feature of public life" [12].

The academic debate includes concerns from within reform circles that the "abolitionist framing runs the risk of sacrificing too many reforms that would benefit people currently suffering from incarceration for a utopia that will ultimately not materialize" [12].
