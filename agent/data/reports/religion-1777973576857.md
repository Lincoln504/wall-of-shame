## The Imposition of Religious Doctrine on Public Law and the Erosion of Secular Governance

### Executive Summary
The legal landscape of the United States has undergone a fundamental transformation from a regime of "separationism" to one of "structural preferentialism." This shift is characterized by a synergistic relationship between the Establishment Clause and the Free Exercise Clause, where the government is increasingly required to provide religious entities with equal access to public funding while simultaneously granting them expansive exemptions from secular regulations [1]. This "structural preferentialism" provides religious organizations—particularly those aligned with conservative social values—with a unique legal status: they can demand taxpayer-funded benefits (such as school vouchers and mandatory funding for religious education) but can opt out of the administrative, civil rights, and public health regulations that apply to secular actors [1, 3, 31].

Key drivers of this erosion include intense political polarization regarding reproductive rights and LGBTQ+ equality, the rise of Christian nationalism, and a shifting political economy of school choice [1, 10, 12]. This has resulted in a legal environment where religious liberty is frequently utilized to bypass anti-discrimination protections, reshape public education curricula, and expand the influence of religious doctrine in the public square [2, 3, 4, 6].

### Findings

#### Structural Preferentialism and Constitutional Evolution
- **Definition of Structural Preferentialism**: The current judicial regime mandates that religious groups receive "equal funding without equal regulation" [1]. While the government must treat religion equally regarding public benefits, it must also grant special exemptions from burdens that would otherwise apply to secular entities [1].
- **The Shift from Separationism to Preferentialism**: 
    - **Postwar Pluralism (1940–1963)**: Characterized by an increasing diversity of faiths and the initial incorporation of the Religion Clauses [1, 32].
    - **Separationism (1963–1990)**: Defined by the *Lemon* test, which sought to prevent government entanglement with religion and ensure secular purposes for laws [1, 37].
    - **Deferentialism (1990–2020)**: The Rehnquist Court's *Smith* decision reduced the need for religious exemptions from neutral, generally applicable laws [1, 316].
    - **Preferentialism (2020–Present)**: The current era, where the Supreme Court has moved toward a model that favors religious actors in both funding and exemptions [1, 317].
- **Erosion of the "Play in the Joints" Doctrine**: Previously, states had discretion to maintain separationist values through "play in the joints"—the ability to limit funding to secular entities to avoid entanglement [1, 47]. Recent rulings like *Espinoza* and *Carson* have largely extinguished this discretion, making religious funding mandatory if secular funding is provided [1, 48].

#### Weaponized Liberty and Civil Rights Exemptions
- **Exemption from Anti-Discrimination Laws**: Religious organizations are increasingly using the Free Exercise Clause to bypass laws governing employment, housing, and public accommodations [1, 13].
    - **Employment**: The "ministerial exception" allows religious institutions to bypass employment discrimination laws for certain roles, an area that now covers as many as two million employees [1, 100]. Furthermore, the *Hobby Lobby* decision extended religious liberty protections to for-profit corporations [1, 110].
    - **Public Accommodations**: Cases such as *303 Creative* have allowed service providers to refuse services to LGBTQ+ individuals based on religious or speech-based objections [1, 514].
    - **Healthcare and Reproductive Rights**: The *Little Sisters of the Poor* decision allowed religious nonprofits to be exempted from mandates, such as contraceptive coverage, even when such exemptions resulted in third-party harm [1, 118].
- **Impact on LGBTQ+ and Minority Rights**: The expansion of religious exemptions is frequently used to challenge protections for LGBTQ+ individuals, including rights regarding gender identity and marriage equality [1, 426].

#### Theocratic Policy and Public Education
- **Sectarian Integration in Schools**: There is an increasing push to integrate religious curriculum into public education [4].
    - **Curriculum Disputes**: Significant legal battles exist regarding "opt-out" rights for parents. In Montgomery County, MD, parents have faced refusal of graduation rights when seeking to opt their children out of LGBTQ-inclusive health instruction [2, 38].
    - **School Choice and Funding**: The *Carson v. Makin* decision has paved the way for mandatory public funding of religious schools via vouchers or charter school models [1, 362, 497].
- **Religious Rhetoric in Policy**: Legislative agendas in several states are being influenced by religious groups seeking to align law with biblical values, particularly regarding sex, gender, and reproductive rights [1, 4].

#### Christian Nationalism and Sectarian Bias
- **Political Polarization and Identity**: The fusion of religious and political identities has intensified. Political polarization has exacerbated conflicts over abortion, LGBTQ+ rights, and Christian nationalist identity [1, 401].
- **Christian Nationalism**: This movement frames political action as spiritual warfare and seeks to institutionalize conservative Christian ideology within government institutions [1, 7]. It has been linked to increased hostility toward religious minorities, particularly Muslims [1, 432].

#### Hate Crimes and Attacks on Religious Institutions
- **Escalating Violence**: There has been a documented increase in violence and vandalism directed at religious institutions [1, 84].
    - **Catholic Institutions**: From 2020 to mid-2025, hundreds of reported attacks including arson, desecration of sacred objects, and property damage have targeted Catholic churches and parishes [1, 118, 41].
    - **Jewish and Muslim Communities**: The Jewish community has seen a rise in antisemitic incidents, often linked to the Israel-Hamas conflict [1, 87]. Similarly, Muslim communities face ongoing threats and hate-motivated violence [1, 92].
    - **Sikh and Hindu Communities**: Sikhs continue to be frequent targets of bias incidents and violence [1, 97], and Hindu temples have also faced vandalism [1, 100].
