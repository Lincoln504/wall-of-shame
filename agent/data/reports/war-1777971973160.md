## Normalization of Military Violence and Arms Industry Interests

### Executive Summary
The normalization of military violence is achieved through a multi-faceted approach involving the linguistic minimization of civilian harm, the strategic laundering of arms industry interests as economic growth drivers, and the rapid expansion of domestic militarization. Investigations reveal that "precision strike" and "collateral damage" terminology is used to rationalize civilian deaths [2, 6]. Concurrently, the defense industry is frequently framed as an "engine for growth" or a "path to prosperity," despite economic analyses showing that defense spending provides significantly lower GDP returns and fewer jobs per dollar compared to civilian infrastructure or green energy investments [1, 3]. Domestically, militarization is accelerating through three primary pathways: the 1033 Program (transferring $7.6 billion in military equipment to police), the massive expansion of ICE (projected $75 billion through 2029), and the deployment of the National Guard into urban policing contexts [2]. These processes create profound opportunity costs, diverting funds from community-based safety, workforce development, and climate resilience toward enforcement and military-style policing [2]. Additionally, organizations like the National Endowment for Democracy (NED) have been identified as "soft power" tools that can facilitate regime change under the guise of "democracy promotion" [10].

### Findings

#### Arms Industry PR and Economic Laundering
- **The Prosperity Myth**: Government narratives often frame increased military spending as a route to "jobs, wages and growth" [1]. However, research indicates that defense spending is among the least effective forms of government stimulus for economic growth [1]. 
- **Low Economic Returns**: A RAND Corporation review found that defense spending in the US produced between $0.60 and $1.20 in GDP per dollar, whereas civilian infrastructure produced at least $1.50 [1].
- **Job Creation Disparities**: Defence contracts are poor job creators compared to other sectors. For example, the net-zero sector supports 667,000 full-time equivalent jobs, while a recent £1.5 billion munitions investment is projected to support only approximately 1,000 jobs—a cost of £1.5 million per job [1].
- **Upward Wealth Redistribution**: Rather than benefiting working people, defense spending often results in upward redistribution; between 2012/13 and 2021/22, the UK MOD's top five contractors paid £15 billion to their shareholders, primarily international investment firms [1].

#### Harm Minimization and Linguistic Framing
- **Precision Strike Paradox**: The use of "precision strike" language can be a myth; if precision strikes require subsequent land operations to be effective, they do not necessarily decrease civilian casualties or collateral damage in conflict zones [2].
- **Linguistic Obscuration**: The term "collateral damage" is used to describe civilian deaths in military operations, a linguistic manipulation that strips away the human cost of war [5].
- **Rationalization of Killing**: Sophisticated targeting and "precision" capabilities have been shown to undermine the moral role of intention in international law, aiding in the rationalization of killing [1].

#### Domestic Militarization and Policy Pathways
- **The 1033 Program**: Since 1997, the DoD has transferred $7.6 billion in military surplus to over 8,200 law enforcement agencies [2]. This includes controlled equipment like assault rifles and over 1,000 MRAP (Mine-Resistant Ambush Protected) vehicles [2].
- **ICE Expansion**: Under the Trump administration, ICE is projected to receive $75 billion through 2029, with $45 billion for detention infrastructure [2]. This scale aims to enable the detention of over 100,000 individuals daily—an 800% increase from historical levels [2].
- **National Guard Urbanization**: There is a growing trend of deploying the National Guard into domestic cities (e.g., Washington D.C., Chicago, and Memphis) for "crime-fighting" or "beautification," which blurs the distinction between military and civilian police authority [2].
- **Resource Diversion (Opportunity Costs)**: Militarization represents a massive budget choice. The $75 billion ICE allocation is 7 to 150 times larger than the combined annual federal funding for job training, displaced worker support, and community violence prevention [2].

#### Hawk Propaganda and Regime Change
- **Soft Power and NED**: The National Endowment for Democracy (NED) is described by critics as a "soft power arm" of US foreign policy that channels government funds to opposition groups to meddle in the internal affairs of targeted countries (e.g., Venezuela, Nicaragua, Cuba) under the guise of "democracy promotion" [10].
- **Regime Change Tools**: Critics argue that the NED's "democracy promotion" can act as a rebrand of CIA covert operations, providing "plausible deniability" for the US government when supporting groups that facilitate regime change [10].
