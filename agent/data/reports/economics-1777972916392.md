## The Legitimation of Extreme Wealth Concentration and Regressive Economic Policy

### Executive Summary
This report investigates the various ideological and policy-driven mechanisms used to legitimize extreme wealth concentration and promote regressive economic policies. Findings reveal a multifaceted approach spanning tax policy, corporate behavior, and regulatory philosophy. Key themes include the defense of "supply-side" economics and the Laffer curve to justify tax cuts, the framing of billionaire wealth as a meritocratic achievement, the justification of corporate subsidies (often termed "corporate welfare") over social spending, and the defense of market concentration and deregulation as drivers of economic efficiency. Additionally, the report examines how corporate practices, such as stock buybacks, are used to prioritize shareholder value over worker investment and how regulatory costs are frequently framed to justify large-scale deregulation.

### Findings

#### Trickle-Down and Supply-Side Economics
- **Laffer Curve and Tax Cuts**: Proponents of supply-side economics argue that lower tax rates can spur economic activity and potentially increase tax revenue by reaching the optimal point on the Laffer curve [5]. However, critics argue that tax cuts financed by massive borrowing and money-printing are recipes for fiscal demise [1].
- **Regressive Taxation**: Regressive tax policies—where the effective tax rate diminishes as income increases—are often framed through debates on "fairness" and "economic incentives" [2, 5]. Some arguments suggest that indexing capital gains taxes to inflation is a "fair tax cut," while opponents view it as a measure that disproportionately benefits the wealthy and increases national debt [5, 6].
- **Opposition to Progressive Taxation**: Progressive taxation is defended as a means to ensure those with higher incomes contribute more in proportion to their revenue, whereas regressive systems shift fiscal pressure to lower-income individuals [9].

#### Billionaire Defense and Meritocracy
- **Philosophical Defenses**: Defenses of extreme wealth concentration often focus on the idea that wealth and virtue can coexist, and that billionaires play essential roles in society [5]. Arguments include the claim that extreme private wealth accelerates innovation, enables targeted philanthropy, and can outperform government in solving social problems [10].
- **Wealth as "Earned" vs. "Theft"**: There is significant legal and political tension regarding wealth taxes. Some political figures and billionaires have framed wealth taxes as "asset seizure" or "theft" [7]. Conversely, proponents of wealth taxes argue they are necessary to address growing disparities and ensure political equality [1, 2].

#### Corporate Welfare and Subsidies
- **Subsidies vs. Social Spending**: Large-scale federal subsidies to businesses are often critiqued as "corporate welfare." For example, some studies claim that federal subsidies to U.S. businesses cost taxpayers nearly $100 billion annually [2]. Critics argue these subsidies undermine the free enterprise system and corrupt the political system by favoring well-connected firms [2].
- **Justification for Subsidies**: Proponents of industrial policy argue that government aid is necessary to preserve jobs, subsidize research, counteract foreign subsidies, and maintain "strategic industries" vital to national security [2].
- **Inefficiency of Subsidies**: Some analyses suggest that corporate subsidies can have a negative rate of return for taxpayers and that the savings from eliminating them could instead be used to cut personal or corporate income taxes [2].

#### Monopoly, Antitrust, and Deregulation
- **Market Concentration**: There is an ongoing debate regarding whether market concentration is a sign of "economic efficiency" or a threat to competition. Some view antitrust enforcement as a potential "government overreach" that can stifle innovation or business certainty [11].
- **Deregulation as Economic Growth**: The Trump administration (2025) has championed a "10-to-1 regulatory budget," requiring ten rules to be eliminated for every new rule issued [10]. This policy is framed as a way to reduce compliance costs (estimated by some as high as $5.79 trillion in "true" costs for certain periods), lower inflation, and boost GDP growth [10].
- **Regulatory Costs**: Proponents of deregulation argue that excessive regulation acts as a "hidden tax," increasing consumer prices, creating barriers to entry for small businesses, and discouraging investment [10].

#### Buybacks and Executive Compensation
- **Stock Buybacks**: Stock buybacks are criticized for diverting resources from worker pay, innovation, and productive capacity to inflate share prices [1]. In 2025, S&P 500 buybacks reached a quarterly record of $293 billion [8]. 
- **Executive Pay Gaps**: Critics argue that stock buybacks are used to "juice" executive compensation, as CEO pay is often heavily tied to equity-based incentives and earnings per share targets [1]. Conversely, some industry perspectives argue that executive pay is aligned with performance and investor sentiment [3].
